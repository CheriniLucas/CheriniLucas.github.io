Comparison of methods for solving systems of equations!

As an engineering student, my biggest problem is the time it takes me to solve a system of equations manually during exams, since these are not the point of interest, but a mandatory step to solve the real problem.

In my search for a faster method, I found three worth mentioning:

Cramer's rule

It is a theorem of linear algebra that gives the solution of a linear system of equations in terms of determinants. It is named after Gabriel Cramer (1704-1752), who published the rule in his Introduction à l'analyse des lignes courbes algébriques of 1750.

Cramer's rule is of theoretical importance because it gives an explicit expression for the solution of the system. However, for systems of linear equations with more than three equations, its application to solve it is excessively expensive. However, since it is not necessary to pivot matrices, it is more efficient than Gaussian elimination for small matrices, particularly when SIMD operations are used.

Knowing this, I can already assume that it will not be the best method for solving my system, but I am going to try it anyway to compare how it develops.

So, if Ax=b is a system of equations, A is the coefficient matrix of the system, x=(x1, …, xn) is the column vector of the unknowns, and b is the column vector of the independent terms, then the solution to the system looks like this:

Where Aj is the matrix resulting from replacing the j-th column of A by the column vector b. Note that for the system to be determined compatible, the determinant of matrix A must be non-zero.

To obtain the determinants in matrices larger than 3x3, the Laplace Development is used to reduce them, and once a 3x3 system is reached, the Sarrus Rule is used.

Gaussian method

Named after Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777-1855), it is an algorithm used to transform the matrix of a system of linear equations to an upper triangular matrix by reducing the system to another equivalent in which each new equation has one unknown less than the previous one, to finally obtain the solutions.

The method consists of:

1- Go to the first term of the first row.

2- If you have a zero in this column, swap it with one that doesn't.

3- Get zeros below this leading element by adding appropriate multiples of the top row to the rows below it.

4- Go to the next column of the next row and repeat the above process with the remaining submatrix.

5- Repeat with the rest of the rows (at this point the matrix is in staggered form)

Once the staggered form is obtained, it follows that the last value of the extended part is the result of the last unknown. With this value, the remaining unknowns are calculated upwards.

Vega method

I have not been able to find official information about this method, so I don't know its real name or original author; and therefore should be used at your discretion. In my opinion I can say that the method has worked correctly for me in the years that I have been applying it.

This method was published on July 23, 2016 on YouTube by Mechatronic Engineer Héctor Manual Vega, professor at the University of San Buenaventura, Colombia. video on YouTube

The method consists, like Gauss's, in a reduction of the original matrix, but making use of the calculation of determinants. The calculation process takes the first value of the matrix (a11) as a pivot, which will be multiplied by a value that has remained within the imaginary reduced matrix (without row a1j and column ai1). The product of a1j and ai1 is subtracted from this value, where i and j correspond to the selected value within the imaginary matrix. We will call this result bij. By repeating this procedure with all the values of the imaginary matrix, an equivalent matrix of one degree less will be formed. This reduction must be repeated as many times as we have unknowns. In the end we will obtain a simple equation from which the last variable is cleared, and with this we will be able to determine the others by returning through the already calculated reduced matrices.

Already with the results, I was very surprised to see how slow Cramer's rule becomes for matrices larger than 3x3, it was much more than I expected, so I abandoned the resolution when I had approximately 1/6 of the resolution of the first determinant at 52 minutes; and being 7 the determinants that he had to solve.

In case you are interested, how I got the estimated time was as follows:

22min(Laplace 5x5)+17min(Laplace 4x4)*6+13min(Laplace 3x3 + Sarrus)*24 = 436min/det

436min/det * 7det = 3052 minutes → about 51 hours

This is how long it would take me to solve a complete 6x6 matrix by this method. It should be noted that in my example I took advantage of the existence of empty values to reduce the resolution time to 298 minutes per determinant, giving the total time the 2086 minutes described above.

In the traditional way it was what was expected, I solved the exercise in 114min, it was a long and tedious process, where you have to be careful not to confuse any value and sign.

With the Gauss method, you save a lot of time, it is easy to carry out without getting lost and it does not generate much error (around 0.4%).

Finally, the Vega method. By far my favorite on the list, it is very fast to solve, it is easy to carry out and it practically does not introduce errors (just under 0.1%), adding that during the process it generates equivalent matrices with one degree less per step, giving the possibility of using the resolution of equations of a simple scientific calculator when arriving at a 3x3 matrix, thus improving the time to 39 minutes and reducing the error to 0.03%.
